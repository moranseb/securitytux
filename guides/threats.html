Understanding Online Threats

Staying safe online requires understanding the basic threats you face. Keeping your devices free of malware, and avoiding falling for phishing attacks is fundimental to staying safe. The following are some of the most common threats that normal internet users face, and how to avoid falling for them. Click the sections below to learn about each type of threat.

Malware

The impact of malware depends on the type of malware, and can range from slowing down your computer to stealing passwords and credit card numbers to encrypting your files and demanding a ransom to decrypt them. At the end of the day, you cannot operate securely online if the device you use is compromised.

While antivirus solutions can help detect and clean up infections after they happen, the best approach is to avoid infections in the first place. To do this, avoid visiting untrusted websites; websites like movie and music privacy sites, or other illegal sites should be avoided as they have been known to distribute malware. If you do need to visit a site that you don't trust, do so in a virtual machine. If a website downloads a file unexpectedly, close the website, delete the file, and run an antivirus scan.

Additionally, do not download or open files that you don't trust. Be especially wary of files sent to you by email, check the senders email address before downloading any files, and do not download any files you are not expecting.

Regarding antivirus, Windows Defender comes with Windows, and is sufficient for that operating system. Sophos is one good option for Mac, or for Windows if something besides Windows Defender is desired. For Linux, ClamAV is a good option. Always use real antivirus. One of the most commonly types of pirated software is antivirus software. Usually, predicatbly, these pirated versions contain malware. If you don't want to pay for antivirus, it's much better not to install it at all than to install a pirated version.

Phishing, Scam Pages, and Social Engineering

Truly hacking most websites these days is very difficult. Users are often the weakest links, and hackers take advantage of this. Taking advantage of users' weaknesses rather than weaknesses in code or website design is called social engineering. Below are some of the most common ways users are tricked into giving hackers sensitive information. As general guidance, be extremely aware whenever you type or paste your password into a website, or enter authentication codes or personal information anywhere. Always check the URL, if it looks weird, don't enter your password. Don't ever give authentication codes, passwords, or personal information in text messages or over the phone to anyone. Hackers will often call or text victims and trick them into giving them sensitive information, for example by pretending to a bank employee. Do not fall for it, this a very common way for people to loose control of their accounts to hackers. Hackers will often use fear to help scare victims into providing this information. Watch out for this tactic.

Phishing is one of the most common ways of taking advantage of users. Hackers will use email, text messages, or phone calls to trick users into entering their credentials into fake websites (see the section on scam pages below) or into sending the hacker passwords or authentication codes over email, text, or by phone. Another trick is to try to get victims to buy gift cards, and send them to the hacker. For example, they may pretend to be your boss, or the pastor of your church, and ask you to buy gift cards for a urgent business need or a family in needs, and they will reimburse you later. Hackers can also get access to an account by using the "forgot password" function, which usually requires personal information as verification. They can then often trick victims into giving them this information by email, text, or phone. Never send codes, passwords, or personal information by text, phone, or email.

Scam pages are when hackers create a web page that looks like the login page of a legitimate website. For example, they may create a web page that looks like a bank login page. Then they send bank customers emails saying there is problem with their account, or someone has withdrawn a substantial amount of money, and ask them to sign in to verify the transaction, including a link to their fake bank website in the email. When the victim clicks on the link and enters their password, the password is sent to the hackers. Sometimes these pages will also ask for the code sent by text message to verify the log in, allowing the hacker to bypass this additional security check if the victim enters this code. To avoid falling for this scam, never click on links in emails. If your bank sends you an email and asks for action, navigate to the bank website directly, and log in from there. Do not use the link provided in the email. Before entering an email, carefully check the URL. Hackers will use URLs that look similar on first glace to the true URL. For example, instead of secure.bankofamerica.com a hacker might use secure.bankofamarica.com. Watch out for these URLs. Don't click links in emails or text messages.

Credential Stuffing Attacks

One of the most common ways passwords are compromised is credential stuffing attacks.

Databases containing username password combinations are obtained by hackers from by breaching websites, or because the databases are sometimes left unprotected. These hackers either sell the databases on the dark web, or use the data themselves. They test the username-password combinations from the database against other websites, this is called credential stuffing. Because users so often reuse usernames and passwords across multiple websites, credential stuffing results in a relatively large number of valid sets of credentials. The hacker can then sell these valid credentials, or take advantage of them themselves.

The two best means of protection against these attacks is two factor authentication and never reusing passwords. How to do this will be covered in the passwords and two factor authentication guides. It is also helpful to use different usernames or emails for different services, especially high value accounts such as bank accounts. This will also be covered in the passwords section.

Public WiFi

Using public WiFi, especially for sensitive applications such as banking, opens you up to potential attacks. People on the same network, especially if the network is unsecured, can run attacks such as SSL stripping attacks that can reveal credentials in plain text. Because of this, when using public WiFi, consider using a VPN. If you don't use a VPN, avoid logging in to any accounts while on public WiFi, and avoid doing anything sensitive on the public WiFi network. Additionally, do not use free VPNs, they make money by selling data about your internet usage while you are connected to the VPN. Instead, use a reputable, paid VPN service like ProtonVPN, NordVPN, PIA, or ExpressVPN. ProtonVPN does have a free tier, as a reputable, paid VPN provider, it is likely safe to use if you require a free VPN.
